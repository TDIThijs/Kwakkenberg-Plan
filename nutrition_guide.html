Why you're gaining weight skipping breakfast

Skipping breakfast is working against you

When you skip breakfast, you arrive at lunch in a mild blood sugar deficit. This triggers cravings for fast-digesting carbs (bread, fries, snacks) and makes portion control much harder. Studies consistently show that breakfast skippers consume more total calories across the day, not fewer — the body overcompensates at later meals. For fat loss, regular meals spread across the day produce better results than skipping.

What breakfast should look like

High protein, moderate carbs, some fat. Target 400–500 kcal with at least 30g protein. This suppresses hunger hormones for 4–5 hours and sets a stable blood sugar baseline for the rest of the day. Examples: eggs with wholegrain toast, Greek yoghurt with oats and fruit, cottage cheese with bread and tomato.

The rules that matter most

1. Protein at every meal — non-negotiable

At 155g/day, spread across 3 meals that's ~50g per meal. Protein is the most satiating macro — it keeps you full longest and directly protects the muscle you're building in the gym. Every meal must have a clear protein source on the plate before anything else is added.

2. Carbs around training, not in the evening

Carbs are fuel. Eat the bulk of your carbs at breakfast and in the meal before or after training. In the evening — especially on rest days — shift toward protein and vegetables, fewer carbs. This alone can remove 200–300 kcal from your day without feeling like a sacrifice.

3. Takeaway has a budget, not a ban

You don't need to stop eating takeaway. Budget it: 2 takeaway meals per week maximum, and choose wisely. Grilled over fried, rice or wrap over fries, avoid creamy sauces. A grilled chicken wrap with salad is ~600 kcal. A burger meal with fries is ~1200 kcal. Same satisfaction, very different outcome.

4. Vegetables fill the plate first

Before adding protein and carbs to your plate, fill half of it with vegetables. This adds volume and fibre with almost zero calories, dramatically slowing digestion and extending satiety. You will feel more full on fewer total calories.

5. Snacking is fine — if it's protein or fibre

Snacking is not the problem — what you snack on is. A handful of crisps is 150 kcal of refined fat with no satiety. 200g Greek yoghurt is 120 kcal with 20g protein and keeps you full for 2 hours. Approved snacks: Greek yoghurt, cottage cheese, boiled eggs, handful of nuts (small portion), apple with peanut butter, raw vegetables with hummus.

The breakfast problem — solved

Why you've been skipping it

Most people skip breakfast because they're not hungry in the morning. This is often because dinner was too large and too late, keeping insulin elevated overnight. The fix: eat a lighter dinner (stop at 70% full), eat dinner earlier if possible, and within a week morning hunger returns naturally.

If you genuinely have no time

Prep the night before: overnight oats take 3 minutes, are ready in the morning, and deliver 400 kcal and 25–30g protein. Alternatively: 200g Greek yoghurt + a banana + a handful of nuts eaten at your desk takes 90 seconds and no cooking.

Realistic timeline

What to expect

  • Week 1–2: 1–2 kg loss, mostly water weight from reduced carb intake. Scale drops fast — don't be fooled, it's not all fat.
  • Week 3–8: 0.4–0.6 kg per week of actual fat loss. Muscle is building simultaneously so the scale may stall even as you get leaner — go by how clothes fit.
  • Month 3–4: 8–10 kg down from start. Knee will be noticeably easier to manage at lower body weight.
  • Don't crash diet: Going below 1800 kcal at your size risks muscle loss, fatigue, and slowing your metabolism. 2150 is the floor.
Tracking tips

Use the daily log tab

Log everything you eat — including the small things. Research shows that people who track consistently lose 2× more weight than those who don't, even eating the same foods. You don't need to be perfect every day — aim for 5 out of 7 days tracked accurately.

Apps to use alongside this

MyFitnessPal or Cronometer have full food databases with Dutch products included. Scan barcodes directly. Use them for looking up exact macros, then log the summary here. Cronometer is more accurate for micronutrients; MyFitnessPal has a bigger food library.
